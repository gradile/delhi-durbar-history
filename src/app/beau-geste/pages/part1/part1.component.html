Chapter 1

I have been wanting to do something for some time now, and I decided to share it with you. Many collectors ask for the history behind the Delhi Durbar 1903 line. I still decided to write a book, or a booklet about the subject, but I seem to never find the time to do it. So I decided to write it in short chapters, set by set, with all the research and the story behind every piece. I will share the chapters with you monthly or every few weeks. This is a very peculiar historical period, a very short time frame of an event that was unique, a festival that was planned for years to show the unlimited richness and the splendid pomp of India at the beginning of the XX Century.
We began making miniatures in 1982. Our first line was called Donzino & Donzino, and we featured Ancient and Medieval periods in metal miniatures, in 65 mm, museum quality. We opened our bookstore in downtown Buenos Aires in 1985. We sold books about uniformology, military vehicles, planes, ships and modelling. We switched to toy soldier making in 1999, beginning with French Foreign Legion (Beau Geste is the name of a novel about a FFL soldier), then going to Napoleonics, and World War I mainly. We sold our lines at the shop.
Among the collectors visiting us there was one who wanted to know if we'd make pieces from the Delhi Durbar for him.
As usual Daniel said that we could do anything ( ) so we began researching about the period, accepting to make figures of the Delhi Durbar for the collector if we could release them as a standard line within Beau Geste. Of course we said yes, so the research began.
Daniel spent many hours online until he found Gertrude Bell's website, with all her photos, among which the India 1903 was a special chapter. Our collector got very excited with this new information, and he chose the Alwar's Carriage as his first commission.

This was the only picture that we had from it. No more detail, no way of getting a larger and neater image. So we had to figure out by ourselves.

This is the first Delhi Durbar set we ever made, the Alwar's Carriage, set 62-63-64. Click here to see the set in our Beau Geste site.

I've always taken my own photographs and my camera was far from capable of portraying the real beauty by then. Those who have the set can speak that. We made 27 pieces so far. It is a complicated set to expose, and even so much more to make. It takes a lot of space and it is relatively heavy. But it is definitely worth it. We learned later that it was in the Retainers' Review and probably in the second group of elephants at the Main Procession although there's not many pictures of it there. Journalists, photographers and writers described in detail the first part of the parades, and left completely uncovered the rest; about 70% of the carriages, elephants, soldiers, bands and the never ending list of participants weren't photographed nor described. They were far too many. Here is a picture of the master of the elephant, and the sketches that Daniel made when he was modelling the piece.

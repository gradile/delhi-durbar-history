Menpes: the camps
'After luncheon the Major took us to Jind, and there we saw a series of strange gentlemen. Sikhs they were, most of them dressed in blue, and bearing war implements and steel rings. They had circles of steel round their necks, similar circles round their turbans. At first it was a puzzle to me why they should carry these hoops; they were flat like quoits, had a sharp knife-edge, and appeared in most unexpected places — round the neck, round the waist, and on their turbans. Were they worn for decorative reasons? I discovered that the hoops were used simply as implements of war. They are deadly weapons. The Sikhs throw them at their enemies, hurl them along edgeways as you would a quoit, and cut off heads as easily as you would slice a lemon'.

At this point there was a group of figures we had not considered yet, but that was really necessary to release to be able to display the parade accurately. They were the lining troops, standing all along the path of the elephants. I am finishing this chapter with the following state elephant: the State Elephant of Alwar. I chose the elephant for many reasons. The chandeliers attached to the tusks. The elegant mahout with the golden jacket. The rich embroidery. But the main reason was that the Maharaja was standing on the howda. I was fascinated by that. You can remember from the films how rough the movement of the howdas was for passengers while the elephants walked.It was hard to remain standing with the instability. Still Maharaja Jai Singh Prabhakar proudly stood there.

By that time I had already bought copies of the Delhi Durbar photos at the British Library. In paper. That was a mistake. I could buy a digital copy of the same photograph a couple of years later (I LOVE the photo), and the difference was really noticeable.

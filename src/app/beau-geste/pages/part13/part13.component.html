Chapter 13

It was 2006 and the Delhi Durbar elephants began to become popular. So we decided to release two elephants together for the Chicago show. Daniel worked so hard for the show that year. We brought two brand new elephants that September: the State Elephant of Travancore, which I featured in my last newsletter, and the State Elephant of Kashmir.
We brought the backdrop that we used for the previous show and exhibited a small Durbar parade at our room. I still have the file of the backdrop image to share with those who'd like to print and use it. It's a 17 years old image, I imagine that it could be much improved by someone who has the skill to work with images. Feel free to ask for it if you want it and I will email it to you.

By this time I was already buying digital copies from the British Library regularly. There was a very neat photograph that showed a bunch of men, soldiers and musicians. They were from Sehr & Mokalla. I really liked their picture specially because they seemed relaxed and having fun. There was no discipline, and they were talking to one another and laughing.

Beau Geste Set 211 - Troops of the Sultan of Makala, Sehr & Mukalla, Delhi Durbar 1903.

Beau Geste Set 212 - Music band from Makala, Sehr & Mukalla, Delhi Durbar 1903.

This is what the newspapers and books said about them.

Pioneer Mail - January 16.
'The Coronation Durbar: Sher and Makullah sent Arab and African irregulars, very picturesque figures indeed in their crude colours and head-dresses but little known in Upper India.'

A Motley Army
'Nor have readers learned, perchance, of the followers of that Arabian potentate, the Sultan of Shehr and Makalla, tattered cavalry and infantry in scarlet with yellow facings and the hooded headgear of the desert, carrying old muskets, rusty shields, and quaint tomtoms.'

1) 46 Arab irregulars

Madras Times - January 8.
'The Sultan of Sher and Mokalla sent Arab African Irregulars, who danced past the Viceroy to music to their own instruments.'

2) 29 Africans and musicians

Civil & Military Gazette - January 13.
'They were black as ink and wore uniforms of dull red and yellow, and they carried long damascus guns and silver powder horns. In front was a green and red silk flag. They danced in time as they went and the musicians blew furiously on tin whistles and wore thick English boots.'

The next one was another elephant. A British collector contacted us asking if we could make an elephant from the Durbar for him. We accepted, with the usual condition of being able to release it within our regular line afterwards. It was the State Elephant of Indore. Stunning if you ask me. One of the most popular throughout our history.

I want to share with you some of my 'treasures'. I don't own many real photos but I managed to buy a few along the years. I don't have the money to buy most of the ones offered for sale, I mainly depend on the generosity of my friends who share scans or photos of their acquisitions with me. But every now and then I find a piece that I can afford. This is one of the few.

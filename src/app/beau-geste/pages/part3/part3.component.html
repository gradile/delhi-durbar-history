Chapter 3

I have a few last comments about the Alwar's Throne (Rewa's in fact). It was (and is) a lot of work to make it.
The poles were made of brass so they'd not bend because of the weight of the palanquin itself. It is heavy, even if it's cast in 2 parts, meaning that it's hollow. The umbrella is cast separated and placed in its place with a brass wire shaped specially to fit. We took it as a new set to the first Chicago show that we attended, in September, 2001.
Our room had a nice street view, and the window sill was wide, so we could exhibit all the new sets there, with great light.
The palanquin was exhibited among a group of other Durbar sets. It took only a few hours for a collector to come in the room and buy them all. ALL. A long time collector who is still today one of our most faithful and enthusiastic 1903 Delhi Durbar collectors, and that is reading this newsletter with you all. I hope he remembers this. I certainly do. That left us with an empty table for Sunday, only with Napoleonic and French Foreign Legion sets. Here's a picture of the table and me, early that Sunday. Not a very neat, it was all my first digital camera (Olympus 1 Megapixel) could take. Still a wonderful memory.

The next Delhi Durbar set were the Jaipur Horsemen. Still we only could rely on with the Gertrude Bell image

Beau Geste Set 72 - Jaipur Horsemen, Delhi Durbar 1903.

I didn't know that they were actually 60 horsemen in the group then. I could have offered accessory sets of the cavalry for those who wanted a larger group.

This is what the program and the press said about them:

Jaipur - 60 Horsemen in chain armour

Civil & Military Gazette 13/01:
'They carried lances with red and white pennants and their boots were Cawnpore boots.'

Menpes:
'In Rajputana we saw real workmen. How devoted the old Raj is to arms! Here were horsemen in chain armour, stern and forbidding of demeanour, clothed in metal from head to foot. The only frivolous touches about them were the golden plumes on their casques and the red-and-white pennons that fluttered on their lances. They took one back to the olden days of battle and bloodshed: they reminded one of boyhood's heroes. How much more awe-inspiring are these mail-clad warriors than our khaki-clad brave soldiers of to-day!'

Wheeler:
'Sixty lancers in chain armour rode with the Jaipur contingent.'

Madras Times 09/01:
'The Maharaja of Jaipur's sixty horsemen in complete mail armour, with lances and red and white pennons, caused hundreds of kodaks to set the work.'

These are the images that we got to finally have until today.







The following set was the Orchha Infantry. Another photo taken from Gertrude Bell's website. Their uniforms and hats were unique, coats quilted and long, hats bulky cone shaped and with flaps around the neck.

Here is the original photo:

Beau Geste Set 73, Oorcha infantry, Delhi Durbar 1903.

This time again we trusted on Mrs Bell's information, and she said they were from the Oorcha contingent. We hadn't had the chance of finding a Menpes book yet, that would have had us corrected. They were from Kishangarh state.

These is what the program and press said about them:

Kishangarh - 60 Irregular foot in quilted uniforms

Civil & Military Gazette - January 13:
'Their dress was greyband shaped like a dressing gown, with flaps down the neck and sides of the face. They carried rhinoceros shields.'

Madras Times - January 8:
'Kishangarh sent a large body of armed men clothed in thick quilted coats designed as protection against sword cuts and slugs.'

Pioneer Mail - January 16 / The Coronation Durbar:
'...and the infantry marching in long, quilted coats reaching to their heels.'

Menpes:
'A group of warriors from Kishangarh wore long slate-gray quilted coats, and quilted head-dresses bound with green as a protection from sword-cuts. Rather like dressing-gowns they seemed; but very formidable and inaccessible were these quilted people, with their shields of brass and spears of steel.'

A Motley Army:
'Near these weird fanatics stood a full company of men from Kishangath, attired in vast robes of slate-blue tint. At the first glance they looked liked cowled monks; then you wondered whether they were nobles in hooded dressing gowns; finally you perceived that they carried swords, and that their dress was a huge quilted coat stuffed with cotton. And excellent garments they must have been, too, for warding off sword-cuts, though rather oppressive to wear in the sandy wastes of Rajputana; doubtless they were the outcome of human ingenuity triumphing over the scarcity and costliness of iron. Their high conical quilted headgear was bound with cords of green and black.'

Wheeler:
'...he also sent men on foot wearing long heavily quilted coats and head-gear - a sure protection against sword-cuts - and carrying shields of rhinoceros hide.'

These are the photos that we gathered over time. To be fair with Gertrude Bell, there are a couple of mistakes in the photos as well. One of the stereoviews has 'Shapura retainers' written on it, and the other one is titled Charkaree retainers (meaning Chrakkhari). The confirmation comes with the program, where they are described as part of the Kishangarh contingent.

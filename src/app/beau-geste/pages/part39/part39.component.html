And now, my Delhi Report. It was such a great experience to be there, in the site where the elephants walked. The thrill to stand or walk on the same spots was unique. There are a few conclusions left with me by the research. I couldn't find any written report of the Delhi Durbar 1903, less pictures or plates. I don't think I will find more information in Delhi. At least, not in the way I am used to work. I got under the impression that the Indian people didn't care much for the event. It was one more parade, a pretty large and joyful one, which they enjoyed at the time, but didn't mind enough to keep a record of it. Maybe that was not something they were used to do on that moment. This is an absolutely subjective opinion, poorly based on my very short experience there. I got under the impression that the only interested on keeping records for the future, the only ones who fully recorded, written and graphically, were mainly the British, followed by the foreign press. If I were to find more information, it would be by travelling from state to state, visiting the museums to see if there is something left there that they could save from the pass of time. But even then, if I find howdas, or elephant trappings or even uniforms, I am not sure they will have enough information to confirm that they were present in 1903. I have more faith in private collectors than in official records. And collectors can be found all over the world, not necessary only in india. So I came back a bit more disoriented than when I went there. I am almost sure that I will find more information in the UK than locally. All and all, this is just my opinion, the feeling that the trip left in me. Time might prove me differently. We'll see.

Delhi has grown exponentially during the past century. It is the National Capital Territory of india, containing New Delhi as the capital city. The old durbar route can be found in Old Delhi, a sector in the north of the territory. Delhi's population is almost 34 million people, in almost 1500 Km2. It is VERY crowded, and I found Old Delhi so crowded that it was difficult to take pictures with the sea of people moving around. It is a nightmare for taxi drivers to come even near the area, and it took me some time to convince one of them to drive me there. The traffic was absolutely crazy. Just imagine it, and multiply by ten. I managed to get to all the main spots of the route that I wanted to visit, and I am very very happy for that.

Let's begin with the first spot where the Main Procession began. The authorities arrived at the Delhi Railway Station. Most states had their representatives in site already, staying at the camps. This was the railway station then, and the arrival of Curzon and Connaught.

The old Queen's Road is now Shyama Prasad Mukherjee Marg.
This street is where the elephants waited standing on both sides, until Lord Curzon and the Duke of Connaught could sit on their elephants' howdas to begin the march.

The street today

The Indian people in general was not, and has not been happy with the British dominance from those years, and that is understandable. The independence of the country marked a before and an after. Maybe that's why they are trying to not have that the Delhi Durbar from 1903 present? It is undeniable that it happened, but the memory has not been preserved. I can understand that.

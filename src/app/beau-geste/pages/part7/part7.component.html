Chapter 7

We continued looking for elephant photos since we wanted to focus on these. The internet was different by those years, and there weren't as many photos and stereoviews being offered and shared as today. Our collector took me to his local library, and we found a couple of books there, Menpes' and Wheeler's. The first one mentioned an elephant he had seen in the Narsingharh camp, that had a boy astride its tusks, a golden howdah and red gold and red pompons. Our collector loved this idea and decided to go for a second elephant portraying the boy. We didn't have the program of the Retainers Review by then, so we didn't even know how many elephants each state had brought to the Durbar. Daniel looked around Menpes book for the inspiration of a howda, and he decided to use the general idea of one of the plates, which we didn't know which state it represented. Here is Menpes lovely color plate.

I learned later that was the State Elephant of Limri.

When the master was ready, it was my turn to decide which way to go with the elephant's clothes design. In this case I didn't have any newspaper reference, nor photos, anything. One of the options was to use the colors that Menpes had chosen, but they seemed limited to me then. As a painter, he used the color he had at hand instead of the real one, except when the color was the most important feature. If you check his plates, you'll see predominant reds, yellows and greens. The right thing would probably have been to follow his colors, but for some reason I wanted our second elephant to be more colorful, having in mind that I didn't have photo nor written reference. I went for colors and patterns that I liked, and I was happy with the result. It is a very beautiful elephant.

Beau Geste Set 100 – Narsinhgarh Elephant, Delhi Durbar, 1903.

As usual, the years brought so much information and we could find the State Elephant of Narsingharh, but I'll leave that to the chapter that features that elephant.

The other book, Wheeler's (History of the Delhi Coronation Durbar) showed so many pictures of the Retainers Review that we couldn't believe our luck. We could only take photocopies of the images we were interested on, but the high definition of the original photographs was amazing. Daniel loved the idea of making the horsemen standing on their saddles, so we decided they'd be our next set. Here is the photocopy we used to make the set.

We decided to make a set with 2 horses. It was not the right decision, since they were really four and the collectors would have to buy 2 sets in order to have the parade correct. But they looked great!

Beau Geste Set 105 – Kishangarh Horsemen, Delhi Durbar, 1903.

I got to get more photos during the years. Many years later, having in mind that this is one of our earlier sets, and as it happened with every part of the parade that was more noticeable than others, they got photographed a lot. Luckily. Here they are.
